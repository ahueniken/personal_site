TED SARGENT

Transforming nanomaterials into useful devices

Biography

Ted Sargent holds the Canada Research Chair in Nanotechnology at the University of Toronto, where he also serves as Vice-Dean for Research of the Faculty of Applied Science and Engineering. He earned the B.Sc. (Engineering Physics) from Queen's in 1995 and the PhD from University of Toronto in 1998. He is Fellow of the AAAS "...for distinguished contributions to the development of solar cells and light sensors based on solution-processed semiconductors" and is Fellow of the IEEE "... for contributions to colloidal quantum dot optoelectronic devices." His work has been published in Nature, Science, Nature Nanotechnology, Nature Chemistry, Nature Materials, and Nature Photonics. He is a KAUST Investigator. He is founder and CTO of InVisage Technologies (Menlo Park) and is a co-founder of Xagenic (Toronto).

Abstract

Scientists and engineers have now gained exquisite control over the nanoworld. We can tune the optical, electronic, and mechanical properties of matter with precision and adaptability. The challenge for the engineer is to transform this new dexterity into solutions that benefit the environment, health, and quality of life. I will discuss three key advances along these fronts: new spray-on solar cells that capture the sun's broad spectrum; light detectors for ultra-low-light image capture; and automated biosensors for infectious disease diagnosis at the point of need.

TODD REICHERT & CAMERON ROBERSTON

Doing More with Less: Human Flight on the Power of a Cordless Drill

Biography

Having been likened to modern day Wright Brothers, Todd Reichert and Cameron Robertson are known for the achievement of two historical aviation firsts. In 2010, Todd and Cameron led a team of engineering students to the realization of an ancient aeronautical dream with the first sustained flight of a human-powered flapping-wing aircraft. For this achievement Todd and Cameron were named co-recipients of the CASI Trans-Canada McKee Trophy, among the highest honours in Canadian Aerospace. As a follow on to their first success, the two sought to tackle the 33-year old AHS Sikorsky Prize: a challenge requiring a human-powered helicopter to reach an altitude of 3 metres and stay aloft for 60 seconds. On June 13th 2013, they achieved what many had thought to be impossible, with the prize-winning flight of the Atlas helicopter.

Cameron and Todd met during their studies at the University of Toronto Institute for Aerospace Studies, Cameron earning his MASc in 2010 and Todd his PhD in 2011. In 2012, they started AeroVelo Inc., a company dedicated to increasing the public's awareness of sustainable solutions to society's most pressing technological challenges. AeroVelo has focused on using human-powered vehicles as a teaching platform to demonstrate innovative and creative engineering and to challenge the general perception of the impossible.

Abstract

On June 13th, 2013, AeroVelo’s Atlas Human-Powered Helicopter captured the 33-year-outstanding AHS Sikorsky Prize. Todd and Cameron will describe the path leading to this historic achievement, and the process behind the creation of Atlas. This will include the Snowbird Human-Powered Ornithopter, which in 2010 became the first successful human-powered flapping wing aircraft. The evolving aircraft design technologies involved in developing these unique vehicles have included Multi-Disciplinary Optimization, high-performance composite structures, as well as many creative and innovative solutions to engineering challenges. Todd and Cameron will also discuss future human-powered projects, including their current focus on high-speed human-powered land vehicles and the quest for the World human-powered land speed record.

ALI KHADEMHOSSEINI

Microengineered hydrogels for stem cell bioengineering and tissue regeneration

Biography

Ali Khademhosseini is an Associate Professor at Harvard-MIT Division of Health Sciences and Technology, Brigham and Women’s Hospital and Harvard Medical School as well as an Associate Faculty at the Wyss Institute for Biologically Inspired Engineering and a Junior PI at Japan’s World Premier International-Advanced Institute for Materials Research at Tohoku University where he directs a satellite laboratory. As of November 2013, he has authored 250+ peer-reviewed journal papers (H-index = 54, >11500 citations) and 90 book chapters/editorials. In addition, he has been invited to 200+ invited/keynote lectures. Dr. Khademhosseini’s interdisciplinary research has been recognized by over 30 major national and international awards. He has received early career awards from three major engineering discipline societies: electrical (IEEE Engineering in Medicine and Biology Society award and IEEE Nanotechnology award), chemical (Colburn award from the AIChE) and mechanical engineering (Y.C. Fung award from the ASME). He is also a recipient of the Presidential Early Career Award for Scientists and Engineers, the highest honor given by the US government for early career investigators. He is a fellow of the American Institute of Medical and Biological Engineering (AIMBE) and the American Association for the Advancement of Science (AAAS). He is an Associate Editor for ACS Nano (IF: 12) and a standing member of NIH BTSS study section. He received his Ph.D. in bioengineering from MIT (2005), and MASc (2001) and BASc (1999) degrees from University of Toronto both in chemical engineering.

Abstract

Micro- and nanoscale technologies are emerging as powerful tools for controlling the interaction between cells and their surroundings for biological studies, tissue engineering, and cell-based screening. In addition, hydrogel biomaterials have been increasingly used in various tissue engineering applications since they provide cells with a hydrated 3D microenvironment that mimics the native extracellular matrix. In our lab we have developed various approaches to merge microscale techniques with hydrogel biomaterials for directing stem cell differentiation and generating complex 3D tissues. In this talk, I will outline our work in controlling the cell-microenvironment interactions by using patterned hydrogels to direct the differentiation of stem cells. In addition, I will describe the fabrication and the use of microscale hydrogels for tissue engineering by using a ‘bottom-up’ and a ‘top-down’ approach. Top-down approaches for fabricating complex engineered tissues involve the use of miniaturization techniques to control cell-cell interactions or to recreate biomimetic microvascular networks. Our group has also pioneered bottom-up approaches to generate tissues by the assembly of shape-controlled cell-laden microgels (i.e. tissue building blocks), that resemble functional tissue units. In this approach, microgels were fabricated and induced to self assemble to generate 3D tissue structures with controlled microarchitecture and cell-cell interactions.

JEANNETTE MONTUFAR

Microengineered hydrogels for stem cell bioengineering and tissue regeneration

Biography

Dr. Jeannette Montufar is a Professor in Civil Engineering at the University of Manitoba, and Principal of MORR Transportation Consulting Ltd. She has done extensive work in the U.S., Canada, and Latin America on projects dealing with freight transportation, pedestrian accommodation, road safety, truck size and weight policy, and traffic information systems. She has served as Chair of the U.S. Transportation Research Board Truck Size and Weight Committee, and president of the Canadian Institute of Transportation Engineers. She has also held leadership positions with the Canadian Association of Road Safety Professionals, the Transportation Association of Canada Foundation, and the International Forum for Road Transport Technology.

Dr. Montufar’s work has involved on-site assignments in Ohio, Texas, Michigan, New Brunswick, and Costa Rica. She has also been the primary researcher and author of projects for Transport Canada, Transportation Association of Canada, Canadian Council of Motor Transport Administrators, B.C., Alberta, Manitoba, and Ontario provincial departments of transportation, the City of Winnipeg, and others.

Dr. Montufar has published nearly 200 technical reports and research papers, and has received several awards for her contributions to the engineering profession. In 2012 she received the Manitoba YMCA/YWCA Woman of Distinction award for leadership and management, and in 2004 she received Engineer’s Canada Early Achievement Award.

Dr. Montufar is also a philanthropist. She created the Humming Education Fund to encourage women with limited resources to pursue post-secondary education in engineering, the natural sciences, the health sciences, medicine, or law. She walked 500 km of the Way of St. James in Spain to raise awareness about the importance of education in the lives of women.

Abstract

The extent to which people walk or ride a bike has changed significantly over the last century. The introduction of the automobile, among other things, resulted in significant shifts in mode choice for many people. While engineers have always been present to provide the infrastructure needed to accommodate all road users, for many years, preference was given to the automobile. Now, increasing expectations for walkable communities have “fast-forwarded” society into what we had at the turn of the last century – in other words, we’ve come full circle to walking and cycling. The difference is that now engineers are expected to better accommodate all users of the system, and not just automobiles. This presentation takes a look at the history of mobility from the walking and cycling perspectives, and addresses the role that engineering has played and continues to play in the provision of infrastructure for pedestrians and cyclists.

PARHAM AARABI

The Mobile Revolution - Powered By Electrical and Computer Engineering

Biography

Parham Aarabi is an Associate Professor in Electrical and Computer Engineering at the University of Toronto. He received his Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering from Stanford in 2001, and his B.A.Sc. and M.A.Sc. from Engineering Science and Computer Engineering from the University of Toronto in 1998 and 1999, respectively.

Parham has published over 100 papers on image and signal processing, and has written two books including the Art of Lecturing. He has won a variety of research awards including the Canada Research Chair, the Premier's Catalyst Award for Innovation, and MIT's TR35 "Top Young Innovator" Award. His list of teaching awards including the Gordon Slemon Teaching of Design Award, three--time selection as the Electrical and Computer Engineering Professor-of-the-Year, and the IEEE Mac Van Valkenburg Early Career Teaching Award.

In 2006, Parham's research in facial recognition resulted in a spin-off company called ModiFace, which applied facial tracking and computer graphics for medical augmented reality applications. ModiFace, which has been listed as one of Canada's 50 fastest growing companies by Profit Magazine and one of North America's fastest growing companies by Inc Magazine, currently is a world-leader in imaging technologies with headquarters in Toronto.

Abstract

We are headed for a world where everyone has an intelligent computing device in their pocket (or on their wrist, or on their eyes). This is the result of decades of Electrical and Computer Engineering research and development, including developments in camera and sensing technologies, microprocessors, touch-screens, operating systems, battery and power systems, and communication systems. But the most exciting part is ahead of us. It has taken us decades to get here, but now is the time that things really get interesting with HOW we use mobile computers. In this talk, we will take a brief tour of where we have been, and where this revolution is headed, with a particular focus on innovations in sensing/imaging, healthcare, and augmented reality.

DAVID BRADWELL

Revolutionizing the Electricity Grid with Liquid Metal Batteries

Biography

David is the co-founder, Chief Technology Officer, and Senior Vice President of Commercialization at Ambri Inc., a start-up company spun out of the Massachusetts Institute of Technology that is commercializing a new battery technology for grid-scale stationary storage applications. In 2010, he received the prestigious TR35 award from Technology Review, rating him as one of the world’s top 35 innovators under 35 for co-inventing Ambri's liquid metal battery as a graduate student at MIT.

David leads the technical team at Ambri to develop the liquid metal battery technology, with a determined focus on creating a low cost and effective storage technology to meet the strenuous performance and cost requirements for large-scale stationary energy storage applications. The project has raised more than $25M in funding, including a prestigious ARPAe grant from the US Department of Energy, and two round of venture financing for Ambri Inc. from Bill Gates, Total SA, and Khosla Ventures.

David earned a BSc in Engineering Physics from Queen's University, and an MEng and a PhD in Materials Science and Engineering from the Massachusetts Institute of Technology.

Abstract

The electricity power grid is undergoing significant changes. In North America, the base-load nuclear generating fleet is approaching retirement, transmission lines are aging, the price of natural gas has plummeted, and installing new electric power infrastructure faces permitting and siting challenges. Moreover, broad-scale penetration of renewable resources, like wind and solar, are straining the grid and hindering further deployment in some locations. One approach to address these challenges is low-cost, long lifespan stationary energy storage, and Ambri is developing a new battery that can change the way electric grids are operated worldwide. Ambri’s technology — the liquid metal battery — was invented in the labs of Massachusetts Institute of Technology by Dr. David Bradwell (BSc Eng Phys, Queen’s U. 2005), and Professor Donald R. Sadoway (PhD UofT, 1977, Time 100 Most Influential People: 2012). In 2010, Bradwell and Sadoway co-founded Ambri to commercialize the technology. Ambri is presently staffed with 40 outstanding staff, and is on route to deploying its first commercial prototype systems in 2014.

PIERRE SAVARD

The discovery of the Higgs boson: the engineering and scientific challenge

Biography

Pierre Savard is an Associate Professor in the Department of Physics at the University of Toronto and a TRIUMF Scientist. He received his PhD from the Université de Montréal in 1998. Over the past 15 years, Professor Savard has held leadership positions in some of the largest scientific collaborations in the world, coordinating groups of hundreds of scientists on ATLAS and CDF experiments. ATLAS is one of the two multi-purpose experiments detecting collisions from the 27 km Large Hadron Collider (LHC) at CERN, near Geneva Switzerland. Professor Savard is the co-leader of the Higgs Physics group of ATLAS, a group of 600 physicists studying the newly discovered Higgs boson.

Abstract

In July of 2012, the ATLAS collaboration announced the discovery of a new particle whose properties were consistent with the Higgs boson. The search for the Higgs boson with the ATLAS detector, from the design of the experiment to the discovery, took 20 years and involved over 3000 physicists and engineers. After explaining the science behind this discovery, I will describe the engineering feats that were required to perform this search, including building the world’s biggest machine and setting up a world-wide computing grid to analyze billions of particle collisions.

STEPHEN HART

Reserve Based Financing - Application of Engineering Principals for Capital Markets: The Road from Engineering to Banking

Biography

As Chief Risk Officer for Scotiabank, Stephen Hart is responsible for enterprise-wide risk management at Scotiabank, including credit, market and operational risk. He was appointed to this position in September 2013.

Mr. Hart joined Scotiabank in 1978 as part of a newly created Chairman’s staff unit, and has held a variety of senior positions in the United States and Canada in both origination and credit adjudication in corporate lending. He was a Relationship Manager based in Chicago in the early 1980s, and headed up the Corporate Banking unit in Ontario in the 1990s. Since 1999, he has been in Global Risk Management; initially as Senior Vice-President, Corporate Credit, and then as Senior Vice-President & Head, Credit Risk, before his appointment as Executive Vice President and Chief Credit Officer in October 2008.

Mr. Hart has an engineering degree (BApSc) in geophysics from the University of Toronto and an MBA in finance from McMaster University. He and his wife, Marion, have three daughters.

Abstract

The discussion will briefly review how the disciplines learned through engineering science have been applied into the financial sector. Specific examples of how determination of oil and gas reserves and cash flow by engineers are used to base key credit decisions regarding reserve based financing.
